Before you start working with photo images on your computer (or even view them), you should really make sure that your video system is displaying things correctly. These pages will assist you in this task by providing patterns to test different things. To calibrate the video system properly you really need some sort of hard-copy reference points, so perhaps the better title would be "Adjust Your System".

If you intend to view or manipulate photo images using the computer, you absolutely need to get video card that can support high-color mode (24 bits/pixel or higher) at a decent resolution, say 1024x768 pixels. Fortunately, most of the current video cards have enough memory to do it. Unfortunately, most cheap video cards won't do it well. High quality (and usually more expensive) cards offer better and faster RAMDACs, which really helps, as well as extra hardware goodies, such as built-in gamma-correction, which are usefull for photo imaging. And don't forget about monitor. This is the part of the computer you see the most. Don't skimp on it. Your eyes will thank you if you get a good one.

So, you got this latest and greatest video card and monitor combo, but the photos you see on the Net still do not look so good. What gives? Well, maybe it's because they are not so good. Check out photo.net if you want to see what does the quality photo scan mean. But maybe your systems requires some tuning to perform at its best. So, what do you do now?

First, make sure that your video card is running in high color mode. If it isn't, switch the modes! Consult your system documentation to find out how to do it. You want at least 24 bits/pixel. 15 bits/pixel will do in a pinch, but don't expect great tonal gradation. Avoid 16 bits/pixel modes, they produce non-neutral grays, as one color (green) gets 1 more bit than the others.

Now it's time to adjust your monitor. All these buttons and dials on it actually do something, they are not there just to impress the consumers. Below are some suggestions on what to do with them. If you know what you are doing, just ignore my ramblings and look at the test patterns. I made them to help me adjust my own monitor, but perhaps they will help you too.

Geometric Distortions

The first thing you should do after your monitor syncs to the new video mode is to adjust position and size of the image on the screen. You can do it either the easy way, using the monitor controls, or the hard way, reprogramming the timer chip of your video card. (If you are unfortunate enough to run one of those so-called "user-friendly" OSes, the later is not an option. It's already been decided that you are too dumb to talk to your own hardware. No use protesting now...) I personally would not use monitor controls for extreme adjustments. Let the video card do its work, and set timing parameters right for your monitor.

After the size and the position of the image are roughly set, you should check if there are any geometric distortions, and get rid of them if they are present. It's often impossible to eliminate geometric distortions completely over the entire image area, no matter how hard you try. You fix one thing and the other one goes wrong. Very frustrating. This is partly the reason I like to fiddle with timing signals instead of position controls. It seems that geometric distortions are much easier to control if the timing is agreeable with your monitor.

Determine System Gamma

If you are into computer imaging, you probably already heard the term "system gamma" used. It is surronded by obscurity and mistique and separates Those Who Know from the rest of us. So what the heck is it?

Actually it's really simple. Monitors, which are built around cathode ray tubes, have non-linear brightness response to the input signal value. Intensity I depends on input signal V by power law

,

where exponent gamma is the same for all tubes and has value 5/2. Parameters a and b are basically set by contrast and brightness controls. Scanners (and other input sources), on the other hand, typically have linear signal response to light intensity. This poses a problem: If you scan something and then display the result on CRT, the image will not look like the original, as midtones will be darkened. To compensate, you have to build in power-law correction somewhere between source and output with exponent that adds up to 2/5.

The trouble is that nobody seems to agree on where to put this compensation. PC people bravely ignore the issue and leave correction to software (system gamma 2.5), Mac crowd is proud to have partial correction implemented in hardware (system gamma 1.8), while some systems go for full correction in hardware (anyone remembers NeXT?). Video standards for historical reasons expects system gamma 2.2, and Kodak Photo CD sides with video on the issue. As long as you work with one system, you don't care how the correction is split between hardware and software, but when you start transferring files from one system to another, the difference in software compensation expected bites you. The whole mess is further complicated by the fact that other output devices, like printers and such, also have non-linear responses with different gammas.

So what's a poor photographer to do? Actually, as long as you know gamma of your system, you are in a good shape. Just apply appropriate gamma-correction in software whenever you want to transfer files to systems with other gamma. However, if you do it often enough it might become annoying, so you might consider setting your system gamma to whatever everyone else has in your heck of the woods. No need to switch computer systems - most high end video cards feature programmable hardware gamma correction. You just have to figure out how to talk to yours. I'm collecting specific instructions on how to set system gamma for various machines. Please contribute if you know how to set yours.

If you did not reach Enlightenment after reading this (rather terse and technical, I'm afraid) account of gamma, try the these links

  • CGSD - Gamma Correction Home Page
  • Fixing Gamma from photo.net
  • PhotoYCC Color Encoding and Compression Schemes
CGSD page in particular goes to great length trying to explain the concept of gamma in words. I'm a physics guy, and find one formula much clearer than ten pages of hand-waving and leg-dragging. If exact sciences are not your forte, you might like their explanation better.

Also take a look at grayscale gradients to test your gamma calibration.

Set Black and White Points

After you sorted out system gamma issue, you should set black and white points for you monitor. This is just a fancy way of saying that you should adjust brightness and contrast to ensure that the shadows do not block and the highlights are not burnt out. Pretty straightforward thing, but often forgotten. You might be surprised how much better images will look after you do it.

Calibrate Color

Calibrating color on the computer system is another hard topic. To do it properly, from scanner to display to printer, you need a lot of knowledge about how color works in digital world, some hardcopy reference points, and a great deal of patience. I am working on a free color calibration software project, but it is still in early alpha, and not for a faint of heart. You might prefer to bravely ignore the issue, and look at some pretty color targets instead to see if there is something obviously amiss. You might also wish to set overall color balance of your monitor at this time. Color temperature of 5400K is what the daylight is balanced at, so that's what you should set you monitor color control to. Some monitors default to much cooler color rendition (9000K and up), which I dislike as it tires my eyes quickly.

Final Touches

By now images should start looking good on your screen, but there is still some fine tuning to be done. Scout your monitor controls (and documentation) for more advanced things that can be adjusted, like convergence and moire. But don't overdo things and try to fix something that isn't broke. Factory defaults are usually sane for these esoteric controls.
